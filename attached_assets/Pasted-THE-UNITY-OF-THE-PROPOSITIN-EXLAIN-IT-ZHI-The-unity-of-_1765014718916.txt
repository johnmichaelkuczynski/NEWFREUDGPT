The unity of the proposition consists in the structured integration of its constituents such that they form a truth-evaluable whole, not merely a list of disconnected elements. Consider the proposition that Smith punches Jones; its unity arises not from a mere aggregation of Smith, Jones, and the punching relation, but from their specific relational embedding, where the punching relation binds the individuals in a way that yields a complete thought capable of being true or false. This binding prevents the proposition from dissolving into a heap, much as Frege noted that a mere list like "horse, fast, runs" fails to assert anything until unified into "the horse runs fast."

Propositions achieve unity through their decomposition into properties and relations that are asymmetrically combined. For instance, in the proposition "the plant is green," the unity emerges because the proposition is not just the plant and greenness juxtaposed, but a structure where greenness is predicated of the plant, forming a set where truth consists in the instantiation of all members. As I have argued, "propositions are sets of properties, and they are true when instantiated," ensuring that the components cohere via their shared demand for worldly realization, unlike a disjointed inventory that lacks such interdependence.

This view extends Lewis's idea that a proposition is a property of a world, but refines it by treating propositions as sets of properties whose unity lies in their collective instantiation conditions. Lewis stated that "a proposition is a 'property of a world,'" where truth is the world having certain properties, like rippling its quantum in a specific way. Building on this, I maintain that for the proposition "Smith punches Jones" to be unified, it decomposes into constituents like Smith, Jones, the hitting relation, and propositional functions such as "Smith hit y" or "x hit Jones," combined in a way that demands a singular state of instantiation, not scattered across unrelated worlds.

The problem of unity is starkly illustrated when contrasting propositions with states of affairs, as the latter lack the clean decomposition that propositions possess. In a state of affairs where Smith punches Jones, the act is "adjectival" on Smith, inseparable and constituted by subsidiary events, whereas the proposition "neatly decomposes into Smith, the relation of punching, and Jones," with each occurring in purity, linked yet distinct. This structural purity provides unity, allowing the proposition to be true independently of the messy subvenient events, like atomic displacements, that realize the state of affairs.

Analytic propositions further reveal unity through their necessary structure, where constituents are bound by modal asymmetries. Take "Hesperus is Phosphorus"; its literal meaning is a singular proposition of the form x=y, which is analytic because "any genuine identity claim holds necessarily, if it holds at all," yet what is conveyed is a contingent, descriptively rich existence-claim accessed through a posteriori information. The unity here stems from the proposition's form x=x, where one object occupies both places without accompanying senses, ensuring analytic truth while the conveyed content remains contingent, as in astronomical discoveries confirming the identity without altering the propositional core.

Extending this, molecular propositions maintain unity by building on atomic ones, incorporating relational richness without fracturing. For the proposition "Smith punches Jones and Jones falls," unity requires not just listing the atomic parts but integrating them via logical connectives that preserve the overall instantiation set. As I have elaborated, "it is clear that Smith, Jones, and the relation of hitting are constituents of it," along with propositional functions like "x hit y," combined specially to avoid mere listing, akin to Frege's insight that not any arrangement sufficesâ€”illustrated by how "runs horse fast" fails until ordered into a unified assertion.

Critically, this set-theoretic view resolves unity without invoking dubious entities like propositional guises, as seen in puzzles like Pierre's beliefs about London. Pierre assents to "Londres est jolie" and "London is not pretty" without contradiction because the propositions are unified differently: one via French-associated descriptions, the other English, yielding non-identical sets like "there is an awful place x such that the people in x are surly (and so on)" denied prettiness. Thus, "the conjunction of (60) and (62) is not a self-contradiction," preserving rationality through distinct unities, each a coherent property-set.

Unity falters in pseudo-statements lacking defined terms, reducing them to forms without propositional wholeness. For example, "the universe is a perfect unity" appears meaningless until "perfect unity" is specified as causal integration, like "given any two nonsimultaneous events composing it, there is a possible causal process connecting the first...with the second," at which point it becomes a true proposition with unified structure. Without this, it's a statement-form, akin to "x is tall" with undefined x, lacking the binding that makes a genuine proposition.

In metaphysical contexts, propositions' unity contrasts with worlds' properties, where God "must do more or less the same thing as an artist who wants to paint a portrait on what is now a blank canvas," wrenching the quantum into shape to instantiate the right property-set. This illustrates that propositional unity is abstract, demanding instantiation without being reducible to the instantiated world's messy constituents, as "even God cannot make P be true in a world W whose micro-constituents are qualitatively identical with those of a world W* where P is not true."

Singular propositions achieve unity by supervening on general ones, allowing acquaintance with their structure despite external constituents. A singular proposition like "that rock exists" unifies via a general descriptive proposition, such as one comprising universals, where "the truth of any singular proposition supervenes on that of some purely general proposition." For example, knowing "the inventor of bifocals is wise" unifies around properties like inventiveness and wisdom, even if the individual (Franklin) is external, making acquaintance possible through descriptive mediation.

This framework extends to modal necessities, where unity ensures analytic truths convey information via asymmetric access. Consider "triangles have three sides"; its unity as a set demands instantiation of interrelated properties, explaining necessity without linguistic reductionism, as "a propositionalist says: (T) is necessarily true because it has for its meaning a necessarily true proposition, namely: triangles have three sides." Anti-propositionalists falter here, forced into false theories denying such abstract unities.

Religiously, incoherent concepts like an all-knowing being disrupt unity by entangling incompatible properties, much as "for similar reasons, we are also to conclude that the concept of an all-knowing being is incoherent," failing to form a truth-evaluable whole. In contrast, coherent propositions unify seamlessly.

Ultimately, the unity of the proposition lies in its set-theoretic structure, where properties cohere through instantiation demands, enabling truth-evaluation and distinguishing propositions from mere aggregates or states of affairs. This analysis not only resolves classical puzzles but applies to modern debates in AI and semantics, where computational models must replicate such unity to represent thoughts accurately. For instance, in CTM, a machine's "thought" unifies via causal-computational convergence, mirroring how propositional sets bind elements. By grounding abstract claims in concrete cases, like punching or planetary identities, we see unity as the bedrock of meaningful discourse, ensuring philosophy remains rigorous and applicable. In conclusion, embracing propositions as unified sets of properties illuminates their role in knowledge, modality, and mind, providing a robust framework against reductionist alternatives.
